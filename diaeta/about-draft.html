Shelley Steward Kuras, NCIDQ

A Sinuous Career Path

Why did you leave? This is by far the question most often asked by new acquaintances and potential employers alike. When the topic of my professional journey arises in conversation, it's never with the intention of discussing the volatility of specific industries or the impact of financial insecurities on early career decisions, ("flying without a net" as the aerialists say). While these topics are poignant and relevant to the evolution of my career, the essential truth is that I am a lifelong learner. Like the scar on my left knee, the drive to acquire knowledge has been with me for as long as I can remember.

A significantly positive aspect of this inclination is my desire for continual growth, consistent improvement, transformation, and excellence. Fortunately for me, these are highly-advantageous attributes in the meticulous, detail-oriented AEC (architecture, engineering, and construction) professions. The downside to the ambitious nature of being a lifelong learner is that the career trajectories of some positions simply stop aligning with my personal aspirations. In a world of balance sheets and budgets, the reality is that some workplaces will invariably require a specific skillset and nothing more. In other cases, advancement would entail stepping away from direct participation in those parts of the work that sustain my passion. Sometimes there were only two practical choices: be content to settle within the constraints of the current situation or move on with grace.

I have been extremely fortunate to work with amazing colleagues on incredible opportunites in numorous industries. I'm grateful for each experience and carry forward the benevolence of every mentor. I began Diaeta Designs as a means of access to product and means & methods information available only "to the trade". Over time, it has morphed into a vehicle for passion projects separate from my occupation. For specifics about my career path and insight into my formal education, please refer to my résumé and education & credentials pages.

And for the record, when I'm not working, I love to travel and photograph the remnants of built environments. In a bizarre way, I love seeing nature "taking back" the spaces that humans abandon. Ruin and decay will trounce the hubris of any designer. It's humbling. And beautiful.

Dungeness Ruins, Cumberland Island National Seashore, GA
